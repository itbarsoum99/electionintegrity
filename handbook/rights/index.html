The Election Handbook

The Election Handbook is providing a guide to voter rights for voters in the US, outlined by laws, constitutional or otherwise, that may have an effect on your ability to vote. Sources for this project include usa.gov, The National Conference of State Legislatures, The Justice Department, and the National Archives.

What are your rights?

    Granted by Constitutional Amendments

  • According to the 15th, 19th, and 26th Amendments, everyone over the age of 18 in the US has the right to vote.
  • According to the 24th Amendment, no poll taxes are allowed at polling places.

    • Granted by Voting Rights Laws

    According to the Voting Rights Act of 1965:
  • There is no voter discrimination based on race, color, or membership in a language minority group.
  • Certain places are required to provide registration and voting materials in languages other than English.
  • There will be no literacy tests required to vote.
    • According to the Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act of 1984:
  • Polling places will be accessible for people with disabilities.
    • According to the UOCAVA Act of 1986:
  • US eligible voters overseas will be allowed to register and vote by mail.
    • According to the National Voter Registration Act of 1993:
  • States will offer voter registration opportunities when they interact with the state's DMV or parallel service.
  • States will offer voter registration opportunities at all offices that provide state-funded programs primarily aimed at providing services to persons with disabilities.
  • States will allow citizens to register to vote by mail using mail-in forms developed by each state.

Reminder

Remember, as a citizen of the United States, you are entitled to vote in free and fair elections. Do not let anyone prevent you from voting at your polling place, and make sure you know the laws of your state before going to vote or voting by mail.

What are the laws?

    Voters may not:
  • Cast a vote in the name of someone who has passed away.
  • Cast a vote in the name of someone who has moved.
  • Intentionally vote twice.
  • Commit any other type of voter fraud.
  • Intimidate or threaten other voters.
  • Other actions that constitute voter intimidation or suppression.
  • Over 65% of states expect some form of identification at the polls. Find out which states with our 2020 voter guide or Election Transparency Index.
    • These laws apply to all voters, including you.

Reminder

In most states, it is not possible to simply arrive at the polling site and ask to vote. You must first register to vote, and some states also require photo ID and notaries for mail-in ballots. To find out more about this, visit the Voter Guide, Election Transparency Index, or your state's election website.
